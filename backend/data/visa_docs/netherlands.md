# Netherlands Student Visa / Residence Permit — Guide for Indian Students

## 1. Visa Type and Name

**Visa Type:** MVV (Machtiging tot Voorlopig Verblijf) — Provisional Residence Permit (entry visa) + Residence Permit (Verblijfsvergunning) for study purposes.  
**Issued by:** Dutch Embassy or Consulate in India (for MVV); Immigration and Naturalisation Service (IND — Immigratie en Naturalisatiedienst) in the Netherlands (for residence permit).  
**Note:** For stays longer than 90 days, Indian students need both the MVV entry clearance and a separate residence permit. Most recognised Dutch universities are "Sponsor Recognised" by the IND, meaning they handle the entire residence permit application on behalf of the student — the student may receive only a "sticker" MVV in their passport and the residence permit is arranged upon arrival.

**Validity:** The residence permit is issued for the duration of the study program, typically 1–4 years.

---

## 2. Eligibility Requirements for Indian Students

- Have a letter of admission (unconditional) from a Dutch institution that is IND-recognized as a sponsor.
- Meet the financial requirements.
- Have valid health insurance.
- Meet language requirements of the program.
- Be of good character (no serious criminal convictions).
- Demonstrate the genuine intention to study.

---

## 3. Financial Requirements

- **Minimum monthly living cost:** €950 per month (as of 2024) — must be available for the full duration of the permit being requested.
- Annual amount: **€11,400 per year**.
- For a one-year residence permit: €11,400 must be demonstrated.
- Tuition fees:
  - **EU/EEA tuition rate (statutory fee):** €2,530/year (applies only to EEA/EU students).
  - **Non-EU/EEA (institutional) tuition for Indian students:** Varies widely — typically €8,000–€20,000/year for Bachelor's; €10,000–€25,000/year for Master's. Some programs (e.g., TU Delft Engineering, Wageningen Research) may charge €15,000–€25,000/year.
- **Visa/MVV application fee:** €194 (approximately INR 17,500 as of 2024).

**Proof of funds:**
- Bank statements (personal or parental), last 3–6 months.
- Scholarship letter (Holland Scholarship, NL Scholarship, institution-specific).
- Loan sanction from an Indian bank.
- Sponsor's income documents (ITR, salary slips).

---

## 4. Required Documents (Exhaustive List)

Documents required by the Dutch institution (which applies to IND on your behalf):
- Valid Indian passport (minimum 6 months validity beyond the intended stay; at least 2 blank pages).
- Passport copies (all pages including blank pages).
- 2 recent passport-size photographs (35mm x 45mm).
- Unconditional admission letter from the Dutch institution.
- Proof of financial means:
  - Bank statements (3–6 months, showing at least €11,400 or as required).
  - Scholarship award letter.
  - Loan sanction letter.
  - Parental income documents (ITR, payslips).
- Academic documents:
  - 10th and 12th mark sheets and certificates.
  - Bachelor's transcripts and degree certificate (for Master's applicants).
  - Any relevant diplomas.
- Language test scores:
  - IELTS or TOEFL (for English-taught programs).
  - DELF/DALF or NT2 (for Dutch-taught programs).
- Health insurance confirmation (or proof that Dutch statutory insurance will be obtained upon arrival).
- Proof of accommodation (university housing offer or private rental agreement).
- Antecedent certificate / Police Clearance Certificate (PCC) from India (if required by the institution or IND).
- TB (Tuberculosis) test results — India is on the list of countries where TB testing is required by the Netherlands. Must be conducted in the Netherlands within 3 months of arrival (not before travel). Some applicants may be advised to do this prior to the permit being issued.
- Completed MVV application form (if MVV is required — handled by the Dutch institution or consulate).
- Legalized/apostilled educational documents (as requested by the institution).

---

## 5. Application Process — Step by Step

1. **Apply to Dutch institutions:** Apply directly to the institution's admissions portal. Most Dutch universities use Studielink (studielink.nl) for Bachelor's and their own portals for Master's.
2. **Receive unconditional admission letter.**
3. **Institution files IND application (sponsor route):** For IND-recognised sponsor institutions, the university files the residence permit application on your behalf with the IND. This is the standard and most common process.
4. **Receive MVV decision:** IND processes the application (typically 2–4 weeks). The institution notifies you when approved.
5. **Collect MVV sticker:** Visit the Dutch Embassy or Consulate in India (New Delhi, Mumbai) to collect the MVV entry clearance sticker in your passport. Appointment required.
6. **Travel to the Netherlands.**
7. **Register with municipality (Gemeente):** Within 5 days of arrival, register your address at the local municipality (Basisregistratie Personen — BRP).
8. **Collect residence permit:** Your university arranges collection of the Residence Permit (verblijfsvergunning card) from the IND. You may receive it at the university.
9. **TB test (if required):** Complete TB test in the Netherlands if mandated.
10. **Register for BSN (Burgerservicenummer):** Dutch citizen service number, obtained after BRP registration — required for banking, healthcare, work.

---

## 6. Processing Timeline

- **IND processing (sponsor route):** 2–4 weeks from when the institution submits the application.
- **MVV appointment at Embassy in India:** Varies; typically 1–3 weeks wait for an appointment.
- **Total lead time:** Apply to universities at least 5–6 months before the start date. The MVV process begins after admission.
- For September intake, admission decisions are usually by May–June; MVV processing completes by July–August.

---

## 7. Work Rights While Studying

- Non-EU students with a Dutch student residence permit may work **16 hours per week** during the academic year.
- During summer months (June, July, August): Full-time work (40 hours/week) is permitted.
- A separate **TWV (Tewerkstellingsvergunning — work permit)** must be obtained by the employer before the student can start working. However, for eligible students, the IND endorses the residence permit with work authorization, simplifying the process.
- Note: Not all part-time jobs will go through the permit process; some employers prefer students with EU/EEA status.
- Internships as part of the study curriculum are permitted without additional permits.

---

## 8. Post-Study Work Options

- **Orientation Year Permit (Zoekjaar voor Afgestudeerden):** Graduates who completed a degree at a Dutch institution can apply for a 1-year residence permit to search for employment.
- Requirements: Graduated from a Dutch institution (or top-200 global university in the last 3 years) and apply within 3 years of graduation.
- During the orientation year, no separate work permit is needed.
- After finding a job, apply for a **Highly Skilled Migrant (Kennismigrant)** permit — requires a minimum gross monthly salary of €4,752 (2024 threshold; lower for those under 30: €3,484).
- **EU Blue Card** is also available as an alternative for high-earning professionals.

---

## 9. Language Requirements

**For English-taught programs (very common in the Netherlands):**

| Test | Minimum Score |
|------|---------------|
| IELTS Academic | 6.0–7.0 (varies by institution and program) |
| TOEFL iBT | 80–100 |
| Cambridge C1 Advanced | 169 or higher |

**For Dutch-taught programs:**

| Test | Minimum Level |
|------|---------------|
| NT2 (Nederlands als Tweede Taal) | Level II |
| DELF/DALF | Equivalent B2/C1 French (for some programs) |

- The Netherlands is one of Europe's most English-friendly countries; the majority of Master's and many Bachelor's programs are taught in English.

---

## 10. Health and Insurance Requirements

- **Mandatory Dutch health insurance (zorgverzekering):** All residents (including students) working more than 8 hours/week in the Netherlands must take out Dutch statutory health insurance. Monthly premium: approximately €130–160/month.
- Students who are not working may use private international/travel health insurance, but Dutch statutory insurance is strongly recommended and required if you work.
- Most Dutch universities have student health services and can advise on insurance options.
- **TB test requirement:** India is classified as a high-risk country for TB. Students may be required to undergo a TB test in the Netherlands within 3 months of arrival. This is free of charge and arranged by the university or GGD (Municipal Health Service).

---

## 11. Special Notes for Indian Students

- **Holland Scholarship:** A prestigious scholarship worth €5,000 (one-time) for non-EEA students enrolled in an accredited Dutch Bachelor's or Master's program. Funded jointly by the Dutch Ministry of Education and participating institutions. Apply through the institution.
- **Orange Tulip Scholarship:** Offered by Nuffic and Dutch institutions specifically for Indian students — check with your institution for availability.
- **Studielink:** Used for Bachelor's applications to Dutch research universities (universiteiten) and universities of applied sciences (hogescholen).
- **Accommodation challenge:** Student housing in the Netherlands (especially Amsterdam and Utrecht) is in very short supply. Apply for university housing immediately upon receiving your admission offer. Many universities recommend applying for private housing as well.
- **DigiD:** After registration, apply for a DigiD (digital identity) for accessing Dutch government services.
- **BSN:** Needed for bank accounts, healthcare, and employment — issued by the municipality upon BRP registration.
- **Cost of living** varies significantly: Amsterdam is the most expensive (€1,100–1,500/month) while cities like Groningen or Maastricht are more affordable (€800–1,100/month).
