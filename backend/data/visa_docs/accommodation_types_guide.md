# Accommodation Types Guide for Indian Students Abroad

## Overview

Finding the right accommodation abroad is one of the most important decisions for your comfort, safety, and budget. Accommodation costs are typically the largest single expense, ranging from 30–60% of total monthly costs. This guide covers all accommodation types, how to find them, what to look out for, and country-specific tips.

---

## Types of Accommodation

### 1. University Halls of Residence (On-Campus Accommodation)

**What it is:** Accommodation owned and managed by the university. Can be catered (meals included) or self-catered.

**Pros:**
- Safety and security: Secure building, usually CCTV, warden/resident assistant
- Community: Easy to meet other students; social events organized
- Location: On or very near campus; zero commute time
- Utilities included: Electricity, gas, water, internet usually included in one fee
- No rental contract complexity: University handles all admin
- First point of contact for problems: Just email the accommodation office

**Cons:**
- More expensive per square foot than private rentals
- Less privacy (communal kitchens, bathrooms)
- Rules: Quiet hours, guest policies, no cooking certain foods (sometimes no fish/meat in some halls)
- Limited availability; often only guaranteed for first year

**Typical costs:**
- UK: £130–350/week in halls (London is higher); annual: £6,000–18,000
- USA: $1,000–2,500/month on campus
- Canada: CAD $700–1,800/month including meals
- Australia: AUD $200–500/week in halls
- Germany: €200–600/month (Studentenwohnheim)

**Application timing:**
- Apply as soon as you accept your offer — halls fill up fast
- Most UK universities: Apply within 2 weeks of accepting; halls are allocated by date
- Preference form: rank your preferred halls; you typically get one of your choices

### 2. Purpose-Built Student Accommodation (PBSA)

**What it is:** Privately managed accommodation built specifically for students. Common in UK and growing globally.

**Major UK PBSA operators:** Unite Students, iQ Student, Collegiate, Vita Student, Downing Students, Sanctuary Students

**Pros:**
- Modern facilities: en-suite rooms, modern kitchens, common areas, gym, study rooms
- All-inclusive: Bills included (internet, utilities)
- Professional management: 24-hour security, maintenance, cleaning of common areas
- Social events organized

**Cons:**
- Usually more expensive than university halls or HMOs
- Less personal than private renting
- Rules similar to university halls

**Typical costs (UK):**
- Studio: £200–400/week
- Ensuite single: £150–250/week
- Classic (shared bathroom): £120–180/week

**Booking:** Book early — often 6–8 months before start date. Online booking at respective websites; some require a £250 holding deposit.

---

### 3. HMO (House in Multiple Occupation) — Private Renting with Housemates

**What it is:** Renting a room in a private house or flat shared with other students/young professionals. Common term in UK; similar to "shared house" in Australia, "colocation" in France.

**Pros:**
- Often cheaper per person than PBSA or halls
- More independence and privacy
- Ability to choose housemates (find Indian housemates for comfort)
- Fully equipped kitchen, living room, sometimes garden
- More homely feeling than halls

**Cons:**
- Bills NOT usually included — you share bills among housemates
- Guarantor required in UK (non-UK resident guarantor is a problem; use guarantor services)
- Deposit: Usually 5–6 weeks rent (refundable if no damage)
- Disputes with housemates possible
- Maintenance issues take longer to resolve

**Finding HMO accommodation:**
- SpareRoom (UK — most popular flatshare site)
- Rightmove, Zoopla (UK whole-flat or room listings)
- Facebook groups: "[City] Indian Students," "[City] Room Wanted"
- UniAcco (platform specifically for Indian students finding UK accommodation)
- Gumtree (UK)

**Guarantor requirement in UK:**
Most UK private landlords require a UK-based guarantor (someone who agrees to pay rent if you can't). Indian parents don't qualify. Solutions:
- **Guarantor service:** Housing Hand (housingyhand.com), UK Guarantor Service — fee approximately £200–400/year
- Some universities provide guarantor letters for their students
- Some HMO landlords near universities accept international students without guarantor (they know students don't have UK guarantors)

---

### 4. Homestay

**What it is:** Renting a room in a local family's home. Less common for degree students but used for language programs.

**Pros:**
- Cultural immersion; improve local language
- Meals sometimes included
- Safe and supervised environment (appealing for first-time abroad)
- More personal support than anonymous halls

**Cons:**
- Less independence (house rules, meal times, curfews)
- More expensive than shared flats for equivalent room
- Can feel isolating if family and student don't connect well

**Where to find:** British Council homestay listings, local agencies near universities

---

### 5. Private Studio/Flat (Solo)

**What it is:** Your own self-contained apartment/studio.

**Pros:**
- Complete independence, privacy
- No housemate drama
- Study in peace

**Cons:**
- Most expensive option
- Lonely if you're already struggling with isolation
- Full bills responsibility (internet, electricity, gas, council tax exemption needed)
- Hardest to find with no UK credit history

**Realistic for Indian students:** Usually only feasible with scholarship/RA/TA funding (PhD students with stipend) or after Year 1 when you have income.

## Country-Specific Accommodation Notes

### United Kingdom

**Council Tax:**
As a full-time student, you are 100% exempt from council tax. Request an exemption letter from your university's student records office as soon as you have private accommodation. Without this letter, the council sends bills.

**Deposits:**
- UK law (Tenancy Deposit Protection Act): Your deposit must be held in a government-approved deposit protection scheme (DPS, Mydeposits, TDS)
- Landlord must provide you with deposit protection details within 30 days
- At end of tenancy: Deductions only for damage beyond normal wear and tear

**UK Tenancy Agreement:** Usually an Assured Shorthold Tenancy (AST). Minimum fixed term is typically 12 months. Notice period: 2 months (landlord) or 1 month (tenant) typically.

### Germany

**Student Halls (Studentenwohnheim):**
- Managed by Studentenwerk (student welfare organization in each city)
- Very affordable: €200–500/month including utilities
- Extremely high demand: waiting lists of 1–2 years in Munich, Hamburg, Berlin
- Apply to Studentenwerk waitlist as soon as you're admitted to a German university — they'll often move you up the list when you confirm enrollment

**Private Renting in Germany:**
- WG (Wohngemeinschaft = shared apartment): The standard setup; find via WG-Gesucht.de (largest German flatshare portal) or studenten-wg.de
- Mietvertrag (rental contract): Must be signed; landlord issues Wohnungsgeberbestätigung (landlord confirmation of accommodation) — required for your Anmeldung
- Deposit (Kaution): Maximum 3 months' cold rent; held separately

**Schufa Credit Check:**
German landlords often run Schufa checks (German credit bureau). International students typically have no Schufa history. Solution: Ask your university's international office for a letter confirming you're enrolled and your scholarship/stipend amount.

### Canada

**On-Campus Residence:**
Most Canadian universities guarantee first-year international students on-campus housing; apply early (within 2 weeks of acceptance).

**Off-Campus:**
- Facebook Marketplace, Kijiji (Canada's Craigslist), and PadMapper are popular rental platforms
- Average: CAD $800–1,500/month for shared room; CAD $1,200–2,000 for bachelor's/studio
- Vancouver and Toronto have the highest rents; Halifax, London (Ontario), Waterloo, Winnipeg are cheaper

**Lease:** Usually 12-month fixed-term lease; month-to-month possible at premium. Ontario tenant rights are strong — Landlord Tenant Board governs disputes.

### Australia

**UniLodge / Urbanest / Student One:** Largest PBSA operators in Australia
- Apply online; usually 6 months in advance
- Approximately AUD $200–400/week all-inclusive

**Private Renting:**
- Platforms: Domain.com.au, realestate.com.au, Flatmates.com.au (flatshare)
- Bond (deposit): 4 weeks rent in most states
- Lease: 6 or 12 months standard; inspect property before signing
- Bond lodgement: Must be lodged with state's Residential Tenancies Authority (RTA) within 10 days

**Regional vs. City:**
Living in regional cities (Townsville, Cairns, Ballarat, Toowoomba) is dramatically cheaper — $100–200/week vs. $300–500/week in Sydney/Melbourne. This is also where the 5-year regional 485 visa becomes valuable.

| Time Before Start Date | Action |
|-----------------------|--------|
| 6–8 months | Register interest at university halls; explore PBSA options |
| 4–6 months | Apply for university halls (most deadlines); book PBSA if preferred |
| 2–3 months | Actively search private HMO if university/PBSA not available |
| 4–6 weeks | Sign private rental lease if going that route |
| 2 weeks | Confirm accommodation start date; arrange key collection |

## FAQ

**Q: I haven't sorted accommodation and I arrive in 2 weeks. What do I do?**
A: Book short-term student accommodation (AirBnB or a short-let) for 2–4 weeks to give yourself time to find permanent accommodation after arrival. Looking for accommodation after you're in the city is much easier than from India.

**Q: My university hall doesn't allow cooking strong-smelling food. I need to cook Indian food. What do I do?**
A: Read the rules carefully. Most halls restrict cooking fish/meat but allow spices and vegetarian Indian cooking. In HMO (private renting), you have full freedom to cook what you like. If cooking Indian food is important, private renting is better suited.

**Q: What if my landlord keeps my deposit unfairly?**
A: UK: Deposits are in a protection scheme — dispute through the scheme's adjudication service; it's free. Australia: Contact the RTA (Residential Tenancies Authority) in your state. Canada: File with the Landlord Tenant Board. Germany: Through a tenant association (Mieterverein) — annual membership approximately €70; they provide legal advice.
