# First 30 Days After Arriving Abroad — Complete Guide for Indian Students

The first month abroad is overwhelming for most Indian students. This guide gives you a day-by-day action plan so nothing falls through the cracks.

---

## Day 1–2: Arrival Essentials

### At the Airport
- Clear immigration: have your passport, visa/entry clearance, admission letter, and financial proof ready to show the immigration officer
- **UK:** If you have a BRP letter, you do NOT get a stamp on Day 1 — your visa is "entry clearance vignette." Collect your BRP from the Post Office named in your visa approval letter within 10 days.
- **Canada:** You'll receive your study permit at the Port of Entry; show your PoE letter, admission letter, GIC certificate, and funds proof
- Collect your checked baggage; proceed through customs (nothing to declare if only personal belongings)
- **Buy a local SIM card** at the airport (expensive) or from a supermarket/phone shop on Day 2 (cheaper): UK (EE, Three, Giffgaff), USA (T-Mobile), Canada (Fido), Australia (Optus, Woolworths), Germany (O2, Aldi Talk)

### Accommodation Check-In
- Proceed to your accommodation (university halls or private)
- Get your keys, room orientation, and check for inventory defects — photograph any existing damage immediately
- Locate: laundry room, nearest supermarket, emergency exit, university campus

### Food and Basics
- First grocery run: bread, milk, eggs, basic vegetables, rice or pasta, cooking oil, spices
- Most UK/European/Australian supermarkets have Indian staples (basmati rice, lentils, chickpeas) in the "World Foods" section
- For proper Indian spices: find an Indian/Asian grocery store (they exist in virtually every university city)

### Contact Family
- Message family on WhatsApp immediately on landing
- Share your new address and local phone number
- Save emergency contacts: university security, accommodation warden, nearest A&E/hospital, Indian Embassy helpline

---

## Days 3–5: University Registration

### International Student Office
- Register your arrival with the International Student Services / Study Abroad office
- This triggers SEVIS check-in (USA), IRCC compliance reporting (Canada), and your enrollment confirmation
- Collect your Student ID card — you need this for everything (library, gym, discounts, travel)

### University Orientation
- Attend ALL orientation sessions — they cover critical information (course registration, library, support services)
- Collect university welcome pack, campus map, emergency contact list
- Register for library access: get library card, learn how to access e-journals and databases
- Campus tour: Locate medical centre, student union, career services, counselling centre, international student office, IT help desk

## Week 1 Essentials by Country

### United Kingdom

**BRP (Biometric Residence Permit):**
- Must collect from the specific Post Office named in your BRP letter within **10 days** of arrival
- Bring your passport and BRP letter
- Check the BRP details for errors immediately; report errors to UKVI within 10 days

**National Insurance Number (NI Number):**
- Apply online at gov.uk/apply-national-insurance-number
- Needed when you start any work (part-time, internship)
- Takes 4–8 weeks to receive; you can start work before receiving it

**Bank Account:**
- Fastest options for students: Monzo (app-based, no branch required, instant approval), Starling Bank
- Traditional banks: Barclays, Lloyds, HSBC — require proof of address (usually university letter)
- Student accounts often include 0% overdraft facility of £1,000–£1,500

**Council Tax Exemption:**
- Request a council tax exemption letter from your university (you're 100% exempt as a full-time student)
- Give this letter to your local council (if living in private accommodation)
- University halls are typically auto-exempt

### Germany

**Anmeldung (Address Registration) — MANDATORY:**
- Register your address at the Bürgeramt (Citizens' Office) within **14 days** of arrival
- Required by German law; fine for late registration
- Bring: passport, visa, rental contract or landlord confirmation letter (Wohnungsgeberbestätigung)
- Receive: Anmeldebestätigung (confirmation letter) — you need this for bank account, university enrollment, health insurance

**University Enrollment (Immatrikulation):**
- Complete Immatrikulation at the Studierendensekretariat
- Pay semester fee (Semesterbeitrag): typically €150–350; includes transport (Semesterticket for public transport in many cities)
- Get student ID card

**Health Insurance:**
- Mandatory for all students in Germany
- Public providers: TK (Techniker Krankenkasse), AOK, Barmer, DAK
- Fee: approximately €120/month for students under 25
- Without health insurance, university cannot complete your enrollment
- Apply online (TK has English interface: tk.de)

**Bank Account:**
- After Anmeldung, open bank account at Deutsche Bank, N26 (online), Commerzbank, or Sparkasse
- N26 is easiest for new arrivals — fully online in English

### France

**OFII Appointment:**
- Mandatory for long-stay students (Visa de Long Séjour valant Titre de Séjour — VLS-TS)
- Register at administration.gouv.fr/ofii within 3 months of arrival
- Attend appointment; receive sticker in passport completing your long-stay visa registration
- Without this, your stay is technically not regularized

**CPAM Health Insurance:**
- Enroll in France's national health insurance at ameli.fr
- Students under 28 are automatically affiliated through university enrollment
- Receive Carte Vitale (green health card) — takes 4–8 weeks

**CAF Housing Benefit (Aide Personnalisée au Logement — APL):**
- Apply immediately at caf.fr for housing benefit
- Students in eligible accommodation receive €50–200/month subsidy
- Even students in CROUS university residences qualify

### Netherlands

**BSN Number:**
- Register at your municipality (gemeente) within 5 days if staying 4+ months
- Bring: passport, proof of accommodation (rental contract), enrollment letter
- Receive BSN (Burgerservicenummer) — like an Indian Aadhaar number
- Needed for: bank account, health insurance, employment, tax

**Health Insurance:**
- Mandatory for all residents working or studying in the Netherlands
- Student basic insurance (basisverzekering): ~€140/month
- Apply at Zorgverzekeringslijn.nl within 4 months of arrival (backdated to arrival date)
- Students may be exempt if enrolled and not working — check your specific situation

### Canada

**SIN (Social Insurance Number):**
- Apply online at canada.ca/sin or in-person at Service Canada
- Required for any work in Canada
- Free; takes 1–2 weeks
- Keep your SIN confidential — it's like your PAN number

**Health Insurance:**
- Varies by province; most have a waiting period of 3 months for provincial health coverage
- Ontario: OHIP (free after 3-month wait); Quebec: RAMQ; BC: MSP
- Buy private health insurance (typically through university plan) to bridge the gap
- Always check your university's mandatory student health insurance plan — you're often automatically enrolled

**Transit Card:**
- PRESTO (Ontario), Compass (BC), Opus (Montreal)
- Load at kiosks or online; monthly passes available

### United States

**SEVIS Check-In:**
- Report to your Designated School Official (DSO) at the International Student Office within first week
- They will check you in on SEVIS (Student and Exchange Visitor Information System)
- **CRITICAL:** Failure to check in = SEVIS record goes "initial" status → jeopardizes F-1 status

**SSN (Social Security Number):**
- Only apply if you have employment authorization (RA/TA/CPT/OPT)
- Apply at Social Security Administration office with employment offer letter + I-20 + passport + visa
- Without work authorization: you cannot get an SSN

**Bank Account:**
- Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo (traditional); open with passport + I-20 + university ID
- No credit history needed for student checking accounts
- Some banks require an SSN for credit card; use Secured credit card or student card initially

### Australia

**TFN (Tax File Number):**
- Apply online at ato.gov.au — free
- Takes 28 days to arrive; you can work without it but employer withholds maximum tax until you provide TFN
- Required for any employment and tax return filing

**OSHC Activation:**
- Confirm your Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC) is active from your arrival date
- Register with a local GP (general practitioner) using your OSHC card
- OSHC covered providers: Medibank, Bupa, Allianz, nib, CBHS

**Opal/Myki/Go Card:**
- Sydney: Opal card; Melbourne: Myki; Brisbane: Go card; Adelaide: Metrocard
- Load at station kiosks, supermarkets, or apps
- Students get discounts on some networks (Sydney Opal daily/weekly cap is excellent)

### Japan

**Residence Card:**
- Issued at the airport immigration counter on arrival (for stays of 90+ days)
- Carry it at all times — legally required
- Register your address at the Municipal Office within **14 days** of arrival

**National Health Insurance (Kokumin Kenko Hoken):**
- Enroll at the city/ward office with your Residence Card and address registration
- Students pay reduced premiums (~¥2,000–5,000/month depending on income)
- Covers 70% of medical costs

### Sweden

**Personnummer:**
- Apply at Skatteverket (Swedish Tax Agency) if staying 12+ months
- Takes 2–4 weeks to process
- Essential for banking, healthcare, housing — without it you're limited
- Bring: passport, residence permit card, proof of enrollment and accommodation

**Student Union Membership:**
- Mandatory at many Swedish universities (e.g., KTH, Stockholm University)
- Fee: ~SEK 200–400/semester
- Gives access to social events, legal support, discounts

### Denmark

**CPR Number:**
- Register at the Borgerservice (Citizens' Service) within 5 days of arrival
- Bring: passport, residence permit (if applicable), proof of accommodation
- Receive CPR number in 1–2 weeks — equivalent to Indian Aadhaar
- Needed for: health card (sundhedskort), bank account, employment

## Weeks 2–4: Settling In

### Finding Indian Community
- Join your university's Indian Students' Association — most universities have one
- Search Facebook groups: "[City Name] Indian Students" or "[University Name] Indian Students"
- Indian students who have been there longer are invaluable for finding grocery shops, dentists, cheap eats
- Indian grocery stores exist in all UK/Australian/Canadian/USA major cities; search Google Maps for "Indian grocery" near your address

### Practical Administration
- Register with a local GP / doctor — do this early, not when you're sick
  - UK: Register with an NHS GP using your address; free for all
  - Australia: Register with bulk-billing GP clinic (no out-of-pocket cost)
  - Canada: Register with a family doctor (may take 4–8 weeks to get a new patient appointment)
- Set up internet in your room if not included in accommodation
- Buy a monthly public transport pass if you use transport daily (usually cheaper than daily tickets)
- **UK specific:** Check if you need to pay council tax on your private accommodation; get exemption letter

### Financial Setup
- Open local bank account ASAP (many landlords and employers need a local account)
- Set up Wise or Remitly to receive regular money from India
- Keep Forex card loaded as backup
- Track your spending from Day 1 — use Revolut, Monzo (UK), or a simple spreadsheet
- Aim to keep 3 months of fixed costs as emergency buffer

## Common First-Month Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not registering with the International Student Office early** — this triggers your legal status in the country
2. **Missing SEVIS check-in (USA)** — can invalidate your F-1 status
3. **Not collecting BRP within 10 days (UK)** — puts you in breach of visa conditions
4. **Not registering address (Germany, Japan)** — mandatory by law; fines apply
5. **Buying expensive things in the first week before knowing prices** — wait 2–3 weeks to understand local price levels
6. **Not reading your tenancy agreement** — understand notice periods, deposit return conditions, house rules
7. **Skipping orientation** — orientation sessions contain critical academic and administrative information

|-----------|-------------|

| University emergency | Campus security number (on your ID card) |
| Lost passport | Indian Embassy/High Commission in that country |
| Mental health crisis | University counselling service; Samaritans (UK: 116 123) |
| Visa issues | International Student Office at your university |

## FAQ

**Q: My accommodation smells bad / has mold / facilities aren't working — what do I do?**
A: Report in writing (email) to the accommodation office immediately. Keep records. In UK, your landlord must fix mold issues by law. University halls have dedicated maintenance teams.

**Q: I feel very lonely in the first 2 weeks. Is this normal?**
A: Yes, entirely normal. Most international students go through a period of culture shock and loneliness. The first 3–4 weeks are the hardest. Join clubs, the Indian Students' Association, attend university social events. It gets significantly better after Week 4.

**Q: How do I get a doctor's appointment in the UK?**
A: Register with an NHS GP using your address (GP surgeries are searchable at nhs.uk). Once registered, book appointments online or by calling the surgery. In emergencies, go to the nearest A&E (Accident & Emergency) department.
