# Research Proposal Guide for PhD Applications

## What is a Research Proposal?

A research proposal is a structured document submitted as part of PhD and some research master's applications, outlining what you intend to research, why it matters, and how you plan to do it. It demonstrates your capacity for independent research thinking — the core skill a PhD program evaluates.

**When is a research proposal required?**
- UK PhD applications: Almost always required (1,500–3,000 words typically)
- European PhD applications (Germany, Netherlands, France): Required when applying for funded positions
- Australia PhD applications: Required (usually 1,000–2,000 words)
- USA PhD applications: Usually NOT required as a separate document; covered by SOP/research statement
- Canada: Program-dependent; many university PhD programs require it

## Structure of a Research Proposal

### 1. Title (One clear, specific sentence)

Your title should capture: What you're studying + What aspect you're investigating + Possibly: Your proposed method

Good title: "Machine Learning-Based Early Detection of Diabetic Retinopathy in Low-Resource Clinical Settings"
Poor title: "Using AI for Medical Applications"

### 2. Abstract (150–250 words)

A brief overview of the entire proposal:
- Research question or problem
- Why it matters (significance)
- Proposed methodology
- Expected outcomes

Write this last even though it appears first.

### 3. Background and Literature Review (400–700 words)

**Purpose:** Demonstrate that you understand the existing state of knowledge and can identify a meaningful gap.

**Structure:**
1. Broad context: Why is this field important?
2. Existing research: What has been studied? Cite 8–15 key papers (recent, peer-reviewed)
3. Gaps: What has NOT been studied, or where do existing approaches fall short?
4. Your opportunity: Position your proposed research as filling the identified gap

**Common mistake:** Summarizing existing literature without identifying the gap. The literature review is not a book report — it's an argument for why YOUR research is needed.

**How to do a literature review:**
- Start with Google Scholar; search your topic
- Read abstract + introduction of 20–30 papers
- Identify major authors and seminal works (highly cited papers)
- Find recent (last 3 years) papers to show currency
- Note contradictions, limitations, and areas not yet studied

### 4. Research Question(s) and Objectives (200–300 words)

**Main research question:** One overarching question your thesis will answer
**Sub-questions/objectives:** 3–5 specific, measurable objectives that together answer the main question

**Quality test for research questions:**
- Is it specific enough? (Not "How does AI affect healthcare?" but "Does convolutional neural network analysis of retinal images outperform current screening methods for diabetic retinopathy detection in rural Indian clinic settings?")
- Is it feasible? Can it be answered with available data and methods?
- Is it significant? Does answering it advance knowledge meaningfully?
- Is it original? Has it been answered already?

### 5. Methodology (400–700 words)

**This is often the weakest section for Indian students with limited research experience.**

Explain HOW you will answer your research questions:

**For STEM/quantitative research:**
- Data collection: What data will you use? Where from? How much?
- Experimental design: Variables, controls, conditions
- Methods/tools: Statistical techniques, computational methods, experimental apparatus
- Validation approach: How will you know your results are valid?

**For humanities/social sciences:**
- Research paradigm: Qualitative, quantitative, or mixed methods
- Data sources: Archives, interviews, surveys, ethnography, secondary data
- Analysis framework: Thematic analysis, discourse analysis, statistical analysis
- Ethical considerations: Informed consent, anonymization, IRB/ethics approval

**Common mistakes:**
- Being vague: "I will use advanced analytical methods" — what methods, specifically?
- Overambitious scope: A PhD is 3–5 years; your proposal must be doable in that time
- Ignoring limitations: Show awareness of potential challenges and how you'd address them

### 6. Timeline (1 page, often as a table or Gantt chart)

Map out your PhD research schedule:

| Year | Activities |
|------|-----------|
| Year 1 | Literature review; refine research questions; data collection plan; obtain ethics clearance; preliminary data collection |
| Year 2 | Main data collection; analysis; writing chapters 1–2; conference presentation |
| Year 3 | Complete analysis; write remaining chapters; internal review; thesis submission |
| Year 4 (if needed) | Revisions; corrections; viva (oral defense) |

**Show awareness of:** Data access challenges, publication timelines, collaboration arrangements

### 7. Significance and Contribution (200–300 words)

**What will this research add to the world?**
- Contribution to the field: How does this advance knowledge?
- Practical implications: Who will benefit from your findings? (Industry, policy, clinicians, communities)
- Why you are the right person: Your background qualifies you for this specific research

### 8. References

- 15–30 references for a 2,500-word proposal
- Use the citation style required (Harvard, APA, Vancouver, IEEE depending on field)
- All references must be real, accurately cited, and relevant

## Identifying the Right Supervisor

Your research proposal must align with the research interests of a supervisor at your target institution. Sending a proposal that no one in the department works on is a common mistake.

### Supervisor Search Strategy

1. Identify your research area precisely
2. Look at the "People" or "Faculty" section of your target department
3. Read supervisors' recent publications (Google Scholar, ResearchGate)
4. Find 2–3 potential supervisors whose work intersects with your proposal
5. Email them (see PhD guide for cold email template)
6. Ideally: Confirm supervisor interest BEFORE submitting the application

### What to Write About Supervisor in Your Proposal

"This research aligns with Professor X's work on [topic] (Reference: recent paper citation). The resources available in Professor X's lab, including [specific facility or dataset], would enable the [specific methodology] aspect of this research."

## Common Mistakes in Research Proposals

1. **Too broad:** "I want to study climate change" — not a research proposal, it's a topic
2. **Too narrow:** "I will analyze page 47–52 of [one paper]" — not significant enough
3. **No gap identified:** All summary, no argument for why new research is needed
4. **Methods section too vague:** "Appropriate methods will be applied" — what methods?
5. **Overambitious timeline:** Claiming to collect global data, run RCTs, and publish 5 papers in 3 years
6. **Poor literature knowledge:** Citing only textbooks or Wikipedia; no awareness of current debates in the field
7. **Mismatch with target department:** Proposing research that no faculty member works on

## Discipline-Specific Tips

### Engineering and Computer Science
- Include preliminary results if you have them: Even a simple proof-of-concept or pilot experiment strengthens your proposal significantly
- Technical methodology must be specific: Which algorithms, which datasets, which metrics
- Reference specific datasets you'll use (UCI, ImageNet, MIMIC, etc.)

### Life Sciences and Medicine
- Ethics clearance is a major part of methodology — address it explicitly
- Pilot study or access to biological samples: Mention how you'll get access
- Power calculations: For clinical studies, show you understand sample size requirements

### Social Sciences and Humanities
- Research paradigm matters: Clearly state if qualitative, quantitative, or mixed; defend your choice
- Positionality: Some disciplines expect you to reflect on your own position and potential biases
- Theory: Show engagement with theoretical frameworks relevant to your topic

### Business and Management
- Case study selection: If using case studies, explain selection criteria
- Industry access: If studying specific companies, mention how you'll gain access
- Mixed methods: Business research often combines quantitative surveys + qualitative interviews

## UK-Specific Notes

UK PhD proposals are longer (2,000–3,000 words) and more formal than in most other systems:
- Format: Usually PDF; check each university's specific requirements
- Research councils (EPSRC, ESRC, AHRC, etc.) funded programs may have specific proposal templates
- Supervisor must typically confirm they're willing to supervise BEFORE you apply (not always, but good practice)

## FAQ

**Q: I'm applying for a master's by research. Do I need a full research proposal?**
A: Yes. MRes (Master's by Research) programs in UK/Australia treat research proposals similarly to PhD proposals, typically 1,000–2,000 words. The main difference is scope — a 1-year project vs. a 3–4 year thesis.

**Q: I don't have research experience. Can I still write a good proposal?**
A: Yes, but it requires more preparation. Read 30–40 papers in your area before writing. Talk to professors in your current institution about the current state of knowledge. Your proposal doesn't need to be perfect — it needs to show genuine intellectual engagement with the field.

**Q: Should my research proposal match my statement of purpose?**
A: They should complement each other. The SOP is about your personal journey and motivation. The research proposal is about the research itself. They should be consistent (same research area, same rationale) but not identical — each serves a different purpose in your application.
