# Health Insurance Guide for Indian Students Abroad

## Overview

Health insurance is mandatory in most study abroad destinations and can be one of the most confusing aspects of relocating. This guide explains what is required, country by country, and how to choose the right plan.

---

## 1. Why Health Insurance is Critical Abroad

India has a relatively low-cost healthcare system. Abroad — especially in Western countries — a single emergency room visit can cost:
- USA: USD 1,500–5,000 for a minor emergency
- UK: Free on NHS (but only once enrolled in NHS — which requires a health surcharge paid at visa stage)
- Australia: AUD 1,000–3,000 without OSHC coverage
- Canada: CAD 800–2,000 without provincial health insurance

Students without insurance face crippling medical bills that can exceed their entire semester tuition.

## 2. Country-by-Country Requirements

### United Kingdom — NHS + Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS)

**UK students do NOT need to buy private health insurance once in the UK.**
Instead, all Student Visa applicants must pay the **Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS)** at the time of visa application.

- **IHS rate:** £776/year (2024) — multiplied by the length of your visa.
- A 2-year student visa = £776 × 2 = £1,552 paid upfront.
- The IHS gives you full access to the **National Health Service (NHS)** — the UK's public health system — the same as a UK citizen.
- NHS covers: GP (doctor) visits, A&E (emergency), hospital treatment, specialist referrals, mental health services.
- **Prescription charges in England:** £9.90 per item (2024); free in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.
- **Dental care:** Not fully covered by NHS for adults; expect to pay £25–250 depending on treatment.

**Important:** Register with a local NHS GP (General Practitioner) as soon as you arrive. NHS is the primary care system — even hospital visits are free once referred by a GP.

---

### Australia — Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC)

**Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC) is mandatory** for the entire duration of your Student Visa (Subclass 500).

- **Requirement:** Must be purchased before your visa is granted; proof required in visa application.
- **Coverage:** Hospital and ambulance, some GP visits, some specialist visits, limited pharmaceuticals, some extras (dental/optical depending on the plan).
- **Cost:** Approximately AUD 700–900/year for a single student; varies by provider and plan.
- **Approved OSHC providers:** Medibank, ahm, Bupa, CBHS, nib, Allianz Care Australia.
- Many universities arrange OSHC through a preferred provider during enrollment — often a group rate.

**OSHC does NOT always cover all costs.** You may still pay "gaps" for specialist visits. Keep a medical emergency fund of AUD 500–1,000.

---

### USA — University Health Plans (Mandatory at Most Universities)

**The USA has no universal health care system.** Health insurance is private and complex.

- **Requirement:** Most US universities **mandate** enrollment in the university's health insurance plan for international students. You must show proof of coverage or enroll in the university plan.
- **Cost:** University plans typically cost USD 1,500–4,000/year.
- **Waiver:** Some universities allow waiver of the university plan if you have comparable private coverage (ACA-compliant plan). Conditions are strict — check your university's specific waiver criteria.
- **Coverage typically includes:** Doctor visits (co-pay required), emergency room (co-pay required), hospital stays (with deductibles), mental health, prescriptions.
- **Co-pays:** You pay a portion of each visit — typically USD 20–50 for a GP visit, USD 100–300 for ER.
- **Deductible:** Amount you pay before insurance kicks in — typically USD 500–2,000/year.
- **Out-of-pocket maximum:** Your worst-case annual cost — typically USD 3,000–8,000.

**Additional Resources:**
- Student health insurance plans are typically through the university's Student Health Center.
- Medicaid (US government health insurance for low-income) — international students on F-1 are generally NOT eligible.

### Canada — Provincial Health Insurance + Private Insurance

**Provincial health insurance** (publicly funded) — eligibility for international students varies by province:

| Province | International Student Eligibility |
|---|---|
| British Columbia (BC) | Eligible after 3 months — enroll in MSP (Medical Services Plan) |
| Ontario | NOT eligible — must buy private insurance |
| Quebec | Eligible if from a country with a bilateral agreement; otherwise private |
| Alberta | Eligible after 3 months — AHCIP |
| Nova Scotia | Eligible after 3 months |
| Manitoba | Eligible after 3 months — MHSC |

**Ontario situation:** Most Indian students in Ontario (University of Toronto, Waterloo, McMaster, Western, Queen's, Ottawa) must buy private insurance. UHIP (University Health Insurance Plan) is mandatory for international students at Ontario universities — approximately CAD 700–1,000/year.

**For provinces with public coverage:** You typically need private insurance for the first 3-month waiting period — buy through your university's plan or a private insurer (Manulife, Sun Life, Blue Cross).

### Germany — Statutory Health Insurance (GKV)

**German students enrolled at German universities must have health insurance — public statutory (GKV) or private (PKV).**

- **Students under 30** can enroll in the **German statutory health insurance (GKV)** — e.g., TK (Techniker Krankenkasse), AOK, Barmer, DAK.
- **Cost:** Approximately €110–120/month for international students under 30 enrolled full-time.
- **Coverage:** Comprehensive — GP visits, specialists, hospital, mental health, prescriptions (with small co-pays).
- **How to enroll:** Register with a GKV provider as soon as you arrive. They'll give you a membership certificate (Mitgliedsbescheinigung) — required for university enrollment (Immatrikulation).
- **Students over 30:** May need private health insurance (PKV) — costs €120–200+/month.

**International insurance before arriving:** Travel insurance (before German GKV kicks in) — your student visa requires proof of insurance coverage from arrival. Buy a short-term policy (e.g., Care Concept, Dr. Walter) for the first few weeks until GKV starts.

---

### Netherlands — Dutch Health Insurance (Zorgverzekering)

**EU health insurance rule:** International students staying more than 4 months in the Netherlands are required to take out Dutch basic health insurance (basisverzekering).

- **Cost:** Approximately €130–160/month (2024) for basic insurance.
- **Own risk (eigen risico):** First €385/year of costs are yours to pay (deductible).
- **Providers:** CZ, Zilveren Kruis, VGZ, Menzis, DSW.
- **Young adult discount:** Students under 18 have different arrangements.

**Before enrollment:** Travel insurance or your university's short-term plan covers you for the first 1–2 months.

---

### Singapore — Mandatory Medical Insurance (MOI)

**All international students at NUS, NTU, SMU, and other Singapore universities must be enrolled in an approved medical insurance plan.**

- Most universities have an MOI arrangement — all students automatically enrolled and fees charged with tuition.
- **Cost:** Approximately SGD 100–200/year for basic MOI.
- **Coverage:** Inpatient (hospitalization), surgery, intensive care — basic level.
- **Supplementary coverage** strongly recommended for outpatient (GP visits, specialist visits) — available through the university or private insurers.

---

### Sweden — LMA and Student Insurance

- **EU students:** European Health Insurance Card (EHIC) provides coverage in Sweden.
- **Non-EU students (Indian students):** Must buy private health insurance or enroll in the Swedish CSN student insurance system.
- Most Swedish universities offer group health insurance for international students — check with your university's international office.
- **Kammarkollegiet Student Insurance:** The Swedish state body manages free basic student insurance at most Swedish universities — covers accidents during studies; limited scope.
- Supplement with private insurance from Folksam, If, or Trygg-Hansa for comprehensive coverage.

---

### Japan — National Health Insurance (NHI)

**All foreign residents in Japan staying more than 3 months must enroll in National Health Insurance (NHI — Kokumin Kenkō Hoken).**

- Students enrolled full-time in Japanese universities pay a discounted NHI premium — approximately ¥1,500–3,000/month (significantly reduced from the standard rate).
- **Coverage:** 70% of all medical costs covered (you pay 30% co-pay).
- **Enroll at:** City/ward office (shiyakusho/kuyakusho) within 14 days of registering your address in Japan.
- **MEXT scholarship recipients:** Japanese Government covers your health insurance as part of the scholarship package.

## 4. Tips for Indian Students

1. **Buy insurance BEFORE leaving India** — many destination countries require proof of insurance at the port of entry or for visa application.
2. **Check the list of covered hospitals** — especially in the USA, being treated at an "out-of-network" hospital can result in enormous bills even with insurance.
3. **Mental health coverage** — mental health challenges are extremely common for international students adjusting to a new country. Ensure your plan covers therapy/counseling (varies significantly by plan).
4. **Pre-existing conditions** — most student insurance plans have exclusions for pre-existing conditions (e.g., diabetes, asthma). Check your policy carefully.
5. **Dental and optical** — rarely covered by basic plans. Budget separately for dental care (can be expensive in UK, USA, Canada).
6. **Keep receipts and documentation** — all medical claims require receipts and, in some countries, referral letters.
7. **Travel back to India** — many students visit home during summer/winter breaks. Your foreign insurance does not typically cover treatment in India — consider a travel insurance add-on.
