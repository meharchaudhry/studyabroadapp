# Financial Planning Guide for Indian Students Going Abroad

## Overview

Financial planning is the most critical — and most underestimated — aspect of studying abroad. Indian students frequently face financial stress because they either underestimate total costs or don't set up efficient money management systems before leaving India. This guide covers everything from forex cards to RBI remittance rules to monthly budgeting.

---

## Pre-Departure Financial Setup

### Forex Cards — Recommended Options

**Niyo Global Card (Most Popular with Indian Students):**
- Zero forex markup on international transactions (you pay exactly the market rate)
- Free ATM withdrawals abroad (up to a limit per month)
- Load in INR on the app; converts to local currency when you spend
- Mobile app in English; excellent customer support
- Apply online: niyo.co.in or through the Niyo app
- Best for: regular daily expenses, grocery shopping, restaurants

**Wise (formerly TransferWise) — Best for Large Transfers:**
- Multi-currency account (hold GBP, USD, EUR, AUD, CAD simultaneously)
- Exchange rates: mid-market rate with 0.35–1% conversion fee (best available)
- Linked debit card (Wise card) for spending
- Receive money from India directly into your Wise account using local bank details
- Send money internationally at very low cost
- Apply at wise.com; verification takes 1–3 business days
- Best for: receiving regular money transfers from India, tuition payments, large amounts

**HDFC Multicurrency ForexPlus Card:**
- Pre-load specific currencies at HDFC branch (GBP, USD, EUR, AUD, CAD, SGD, etc.)
- Lock in exchange rate when loading — protects against INR depreciation
- Load up to USD 10,000 or equivalent
- Forex markup: typically 2–3.5% (less attractive than Niyo/Wise)
- Available at all HDFC branches; takes 2–3 days to issue
- Best for: students from cities with HDFC presence, parents who prefer bank-issued cards

**SBI Foreign Travel Card:**
- Issued by State Bank of India at branches
- Available in major currencies
- Backup option — reliable but higher fees
- Good if your primary bank is SBI and you want consistency

**Axis Bank Multicurrency Forex Card:**
- Accepted widely; competitive reload rates
- Available at Axis Bank branches

### Recommended Setup for Indian Students
- Primary card for daily spending: **Niyo Global**
- For large transfers and receiving money from India: **Wise account**
- Emergency backup: Keep one international debit card from your Indian bank (in case primary cards fail)
- Cash: Carry USD/GBP/EUR 300–500 equivalent for emergency Day 1 expenses

### What to Avoid
- **Airport forex counters:** Worst rates available, often 8–12% markup. Never exchange money here.
- **Thomas Cook / Moneygram kiosks in India:** Rates are 3–7% worse than mid-market.
- **Using Indian debit card directly abroad:** Most Indian banks charge 3–5% foreign transaction fee + currency conversion markup.
- **Carrying large amounts of cash:** Risk of loss/theft; customs declaration required for amounts above $10,000 equivalent.

## RBI Liberalisation Remittance Scheme (LRS)

### What is LRS?
LRS allows Indian resident individuals to remit up to **USD 250,000 per financial year** abroad for any current or capital account transaction, including education, living expenses, and investments.

For students: this covers tuition fees paid directly to foreign universities, living expenses sent monthly to your foreign bank account, and purchase of GIC/blocked accounts.

### How to Remit Under LRS
- All remittances must go through authorized dealer banks in India (SBI, HDFC, ICICI, Axis, etc.)
- For remittances above USD 5,000: submit **Form A2** (the bank provides this form; you fill details of recipient, purpose, amount)
- Remittance purpose codes:
  - S0305: Educational fee payments
  - S1301: Personal travel (living expenses)

### TCS (Tax Collected at Source) on LRS — Updated Rules

**From October 1, 2023:**

| Remittance Purpose | TCS Rate |
|-------------------|----------|
| Education funded by an education loan | 0.5% on amount above ₹7 lakh |
| Education NOT from a loan (self-funded/parents' savings) | 5% on amount above ₹7 lakh |
| General remittances (non-education) | **20% on amount above ₹7 lakh** |

**Critical points about TCS:**
- TCS is **NOT an additional tax** — it is advance tax collection
- You can claim a full refund by filing your Indian Income Tax Return (ITR)
- Students abroad typically become NRIs (non-resident Indians) after 182+ days away — file ITR-2 for NRI status
- Banks deduct TCS automatically at the time of remittance and issue a TCS certificate
- Keep all TCS certificates; file ITR before July 31 each year to claim refund

### Practical Impact

If your family sends you ₹15 lakh this year for tuition + living:
- First ₹7 lakh: No TCS
- Next ₹8 lakh: TCS at 5% = ₹40,000 deducted
- Family receives a TCS certificate for ₹40,000
- You/family file ITR, claim ₹40,000 as advance tax, get refund (if total income below taxable limit)

## Sending Money from India to Abroad — Comparison

| Method | Speed | Fee | Exchange Rate | Best For |
|--------|-------|-----|--------------|---------|
| **Wise** | 1–2 business days | 0.5–1% | Mid-market (best) | Regular monthly transfers |
| **Bank SWIFT wire** | 2–5 business days | ₹500–1,500 flat + 1–3% markup | Moderate | Large one-time payments |
| **Remitly** | 1–3 days | Variable (~1%) | Good | Quick transfers |
| **Western Union** | Minutes to 2 days | Variable (2–4%) | Below market | Emergency transfers |
| **BookMyForex** | 3–5 days | Low flat fee | Lock-in rates | Advance planning |
| **HDFC SWIFT** | 2–4 days | ₹500–₹1,200 flat | 1–2% markup | If you bank with HDFC |

**Best practice:**
- Use **Wise** for monthly living expense transfers (best rates, lowest cost)
- Use **SWIFT from your bank** for large one-time transfers like tuition payments (banks have higher trust/limits; flat fee is proportionally small on large amounts)
- Set up a **standing order/recurring transfer** through Wise to receive a fixed monthly amount from India

## Education Loan Options for Indian Students

### Nationalized Banks
**State Bank of India (Global Ed-Vantage):**
- Up to ₹1.5 crore for premier foreign universities
- Interest rate: ~10–11% p.a.
- Margin: 15% of total cost (you pay 15%, bank pays 85%)
- Security: Property mortgage typically required above ₹7.5 lakh
- Processing: 15–30 days

**Bank of Baroda (Baroda Scholar Scheme):**
- Up to ₹80 lakh for studies abroad
- Interest rate: ~9.5–10.5%
- Faster processing than SBI at some branches

### NBFC Education Loan Providers
**HDFC Credila:**
- Covers 100% of course cost in some cases
- No margin money for many programs
- 14–15 days processing
- Higher interest (~12–14%) than banks

**Auxilo / Avanse / InCred:**
- Specialty education lenders
- Faster approval (7–10 days)
- Higher interest rates (14–16%)
- Good for students who don't qualify for bank loans

**Prodigy Finance (for top-ranked programs globally):**
- No collateral required
- Uses future income projections (graduate career outcomes)
- Available for IIM, IIT, top-50 global universities
- Interest: SOFR/Libor + margin (~10–14%)

### Collateral-Free Loans
Many NBFCs offer collateral-free loans up to ₹40–75 lakh for programs at top-ranked universities. Required: Co-borrower (parent), good credit history, admission to a recognized institution.

## Building a Credit Score Abroad

Establishing credit abroad is important for renting apartments, phone contracts, and eventually employment background checks.

**United Kingdom:**
- Apply for a student credit card: Aqua, Barclaycard
- Spend small amounts (groceries, transport) and pay in full each month
- Credit score builds via Experian, Equifax, TransUnion UK
- After 6 months of positive payment history, your credit score improves significantly

**United States:**
- Get a Secured Credit Card (deposit a cash amount as collateral; that becomes your credit limit)
- Deserve EDU card (designed for F-1 students; uses alternative data, no SSN required initially)
- Nova Credit transfers Indian CIBIL credit history to USA credit score
- After 1 year of US credit history, apply for a standard student credit card

**Canada:**
- Open bank account; apply for secured credit card immediately
- Scotiabank, CIBC, TD all offer student credit cards with modest limits
- Use regularly; pay in full

**Australia:**
- Apply for NAB, Westpac, or Commonwealth Bank credit card
- Secured options available; alternatively, use buy-now-pay-later platforms (Afterpay) to build credit behavior

## Tax Obligations

### India Tax for Students Abroad

**NRI Status:**
After 182 days outside India in a financial year, you become an NRI (Non-Resident Indian) for that financial year.
- NRI status: Only India-sourced income is taxable in India
- Foreign income (stipend, part-time wages abroad) is NOT taxable in India
- Indian bank account interest: TDS deducted but at NRI rates; can be refunded

**What to do:**
- File ITR-2 in India for each year (to claim TCS refunds, declare NRI status)
- Convert your Indian savings bank account to NRO account (mandatory for NRIs)
- Keep NRE account for funds you bring back to India (exempt from Indian tax)

### Abroad Tax

**UK:** Personal allowance £12,570 (2024/25) — most students earn below this; file Self Assessment if you earned above threshold or received multiple income sources.

**USA:** All F-1 students must file Form 8843 annually, even with no income. File Form 1040-NR if you had US-source income. Deadline: April 15.

**Canada:** File T1 General return by April 30. Tuition fees can generate non-refundable tax credits (Form T2202); these are transferable to a Canadian resident sponsor in some cases.

**Australia:** File tax return by October 31 for the previous financial year. Most working students get refunds due to tax withheld at higher rates.

## Emergency Financial Planning

- **Emergency fund:** Aim for 3 months of fixed expenses (rent + insurance + food basics) in your local bank account
- **Family emergency money transfer:** Test your Wise/SWIFT transfer setup before you need it urgently; know how long it takes
- **Indian credit card:** Keep one Indian credit card (Visa/Mastercard) with international transactions enabled as emergency backup
- **University emergency fund:** Most universities have emergency bursaries or hardship funds for international students — research this at your university's student services

## FAQ

**Q: My parents don't have a credit card for international transactions. How do they send money to me?**
A: They can use SWIFT international wire transfer from their bank directly to your foreign bank account, or set up a Wise account themselves and transfer INR, which Wise converts and sends to your Wise account abroad.

**Q: Should I open an NRI account before leaving India?**
A: You technically need to convert your existing savings account to NRO within a reasonable time of becoming an NRI. Many banks allow you to do this online. It's not urgent for the first 6 months but should be done in your first year abroad.

**Q: Is Wise legal for international transfers under Indian law?**
A: Yes. Wise is an RBI-registered entity and is fully legal for outward remittances under LRS. Banks process LRS transfers on your behalf; Wise facilitates the process and provides better rates.
